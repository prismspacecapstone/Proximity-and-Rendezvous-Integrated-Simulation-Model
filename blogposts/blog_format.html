Preliminary Air Table Testing: Successful Results

Our initial prototype testing validates design predictions and paves the way for full-scale table construction.

Testing Prototype Air Table Research Update

Introduction

We're excited to share the results of our preliminary air table testing phase. Our team has successfully validated the core design principles that will drive the full-scale implementation of our 1 meter by 1 meter air table system. These initial tests provide crucial data and confidence as we move forward with the project.

Prototype Specifications

Our testing began with a scaled prototype measuring 13 inches by 13 inches, designed to replicate the exact conditions of the planned full-scale table. The prototype was machined with the same hole array pattern that will be implemented in the final 1 meter by 1 meter version, ensuring that our test results would directly translate to the larger system.

Key Prototype Parameters

Dimensions: 13" × 13" (scaled prototype)
Operating Pressure: 0.5 psi internal pressure
Hole Array: Identical pattern to final 1m × 1m table
Minimum Viable Pressure: 0.2 psi (discovered during testing)

The 13" × 13" machined prototype during initial pressure testing at 0.5 psi

Testing Methodology

Our testing approach focused on two primary objectives: validating the operational pressure range and evaluating different interface surface geometries. We conducted multiple test runs across varying pressure levels to establish both optimal and minimum viable operating conditions.

Pressure Range Validation

The prototype was initially operated at the design specification of 0.5 psi internal pressure. Through systematic pressure reduction tests, we discovered that test vehicles could maintain stable operation at pressures as low as 0.2 psi. This finding significantly exceeds our initial expectations and provides valuable operational flexibility for future testing scenarios.

Test vehicle demonstrating stable movement across the air table surface at 0.5 psi

The ability to operate at lower pressures offers several advantages. Reduced pressure requirements translate to lower energy consumption, quieter operation, and decreased wear on system components. Additionally, the wider operational envelope allows for more nuanced testing scenarios and better simulation of various friction conditions.

Interface Surface Design Testing

A critical component of our testing involved evaluating various designs for the test interface surface—the component that interacts directly with the air cushion. Drawing inspiration from air hockey puck design principles, we hypothesized that concave and indented surface geometries would provide superior stability and performance compared to flat interfaces.

Our predictions were confirmed through rigorous testing. Interface surfaces featuring concave profiles and strategic indentations demonstrated markedly improved stability characteristics. These designs effectively channeled the air flow, creating a more consistent cushion that resisted tilting and provided smoother, more predictable vehicle motion.

Surface Design Findings

Concave and indented interface surfaces provided:

  • Enhanced lateral stability during movement
  • Reduced wobble and unintended rotation
  • More consistent air cushion distribution
  • Improved behavior at lower operating pressures

Various interface surface geometries tested, showing flat, concave, and indented designs

Results and Implications

The successful completion of prototype testing marks a significant milestone for the PRISM project. The validation of our design predictions and the discovery of extended operational capabilities provide a solid foundation for scaling up to the full 1 meter by 1 meter table.

Key outcomes from this testing phase include validated pressure specifications that exceed initial requirements, confirmed interface surface design principles that enhance vehicle stability, proven scalability of the hole array pattern, and established baseline performance metrics for future comparison. These results give us high confidence in proceeding with the full-scale table construction.

Next Steps: Full-Scale Table Development

With the prototype phase successfully completed, our team is now focused on the design and fabrication of the final 1 meter by 1 meter air table. The larger surface area will enable more complex vehicle maneuvers and multi-vehicle testing scenarios that are essential for our research objectives.

The full-scale table will incorporate all the lessons learned from prototype testing, including optimized hole spacing based on the validated array pattern, interface surfaces featuring the proven concave geometry, pressure regulation systems capable of operating across the 0.2 to 0.5 psi range, and integrated monitoring systems for real-time performance analysis.

Conclusion

The preliminary testing phase has exceeded our expectations and validated our core design approach. The ability to operate at pressures lower than originally specified, combined with the confirmed benefits of concave interface surfaces, positions us well for the next phase of development. We're excited to continue sharing our progress as we move forward with the full-scale air table construction and begin more advanced vehicle testing.

Stay tuned for future updates as we bring the full 1 meter by 1 meter air table online and expand our testing capabilities. The journey from prototype to full-scale implementation represents the kind of iterative, data-driven development that defines the PRISM approach to aerospace innovation.
